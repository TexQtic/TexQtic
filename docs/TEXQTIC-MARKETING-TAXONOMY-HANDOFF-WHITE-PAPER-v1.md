## 1. Purpose

This paper equips the TexQtic Marketing website team with the platform's canonical family model,
its B2B taxonomy structure, and the exact rules that govern how the platform may — and may not — be
described to prospective audiences.

The Marketing team does not own canonical taxonomy truth. This paper defines what Marketing may
truthfully say in public copy, how interest-capture forms should be framed, and where Marketing's
authority ends before canonical platform classification begins.

Reading this paper will help the Marketing team:

- describe TexQtic's platform families accurately
- describe White Label and Aggregator without misrepresenting their structural role
- write B2B segment and role language that is correct and safe
- frame intake forms as interest capture without implying a classification claim
- avoid copy patterns that misposition the platform's commercial model

---

**Primary audience:** Marketing website team, content creators, campaign copywriters, and anyone
producing external-facing language about TexQtic's products and market positioning.

**Not the audience for this paper:** Engineering, CRM, provisioning, or tenant support teams.

## 4. Canonical Platform Truth — What TexQtic Actually Is

### 4.1 The Family Model

TexQtic's platform has **two commercial families**:

| Family | What it means |
|---|---|
| **B2B** | Full authenticated business-to-business exchange platform for textile-industry participants — manufacturers, traders, and service providers. Members gain access to trade workflows, RFQ, qualified discovery, and partner-continuity features. |
| **B2C** | Branded consumer-commerce platform enabling tenant-operated storefronts and consumer-facing commercial experiences. |

There is also one **non-commercial platform category**:

| Category | What it means |
|---|---|
| **INTERNAL** | Non-commercial internal platform posture. Not a customer-facing product family. Do not describe externally as a product. |

### 4.2 White Label — Overlay, Not a Separate Family

White Label (**WL**) is a **capability layer** that a tenant may be enabled for, on top of a
lawful parent family (B2B or B2C). It is **not** a separate commercial product family.

What this means in practice:

- A White Label tenant is a B2B or B2C participant who has the branded presentation, operator
  controls, and white-label deployment experience enabled.
- There is no "White Label family" as a third product line alongside B2B and B2C.
- "White Label" describes *how* something is delivered (branded runtime experience), not *what
  commercial context* the participant is in.

### 4.3 Aggregator — Capability/Mode, Not a Separate Family

Aggregator is a **capability and operating mode** for curated discovery, qualification, and
handoff. It is **not** a separate commercial platform family or a platform-owned matching network.

What this means in practice:

- Aggregator enables B2B participants to find, inspect, and initiate relationships with qualified
  counterparties through a curated discovery workspace.
- The Aggregator mode is bounded: it handles discovery and handoff, not the full execution of
  trades, negotiations, or settlements.
- There is no "Aggregator family" as a separate fourth product category.
- TexQtic does not operate Aggregator as a platform-owned intermediary that takes custody of
  transactions.

### 4.4 Package / Plan — Entitlement Layer, Not Identity

TexQtic's commercial packages (Free, Starter, Professional, Enterprise) define **what features
and usage limits** a participant has. They do not define which family or mode the participant
belongs to. A B2B manufacturer on a Starter plan is still a B2B manufacturer — the plan label does
not override family identity.

Do not use package/plan names as substitutes for family names in external copy.

## 5. B2B Taxonomy — What It Is and How to Use It in Copy

### 5.1 Why a Textile-Industry Taxonomy Exists

TexQtic's B2B family includes a canonical textile-industry taxonomy that classifies business
participants by their position in the textile production and service chain. This taxonomy exists
to enable governed discovery, qualified matching, and correct partner continuity inside the B2B
family.

The taxonomy is an **internal platform structure**. Marketing may reflect its vocabulary in copy
that describes who the platform serves — but Marketing does not own or administer the taxonomy.

### 5.2 How the B2B Taxonomy Is Structured

The B2B taxonomy has **two classification axes**:

**Axis 1 — Segment taxonomy:** Classifies where in the textile-industry chain the participant
primarily operates. Each participant holds **one primary segment** and may hold **multiple
secondary segments**.

**Axis 2 — Role-positioning:** Classifies how the participant commercially participates.
The canonical role-position values are: **Manufacturer**, **Trader**, and **Service Provider**.

A single participant can combine both axes — for example, a service provider specializing in
fabric design, or a trader in yarn and weaving inputs.

### 5.3 Canonical Segment Vocabulary

**Production and materials segments:**

- Yarn
- Weaving
- Cotton
- Man-made / Synthetic
- Technical / Industrial / Specialty
- Knitting
- Fabric Processing
- Dyeing
- Printing
- Digital Printing
- Value Addition
- Garment Manufacturing
- Packaging
- Textile Chemicals and Auxiliaries
- Machine Spare Parts / Mill-Gin Stores
- Textile Machine Suppliers

**Service-specialist segments:**

- Manufacturing Services
- Fashion Design
- Fabric Design
- Technical Consulting
- Business Consulting
- Testing Laboratories
- Certification Agencies
- Textile Software Providers

### 5.4 Enterprise Is Not a Separate Tier or Branch

"Enterprise" in TexQtic refers to **depth within the B2B family** — such as larger-scale
organizational posture or advanced feature enablement. It is not a separate product family, a
separate taxonomy branch, or a separate customer segment above B2B.

Do not market "Enterprise" as a separate tier that sits above or beside B2B.

### 5.5 Service Providers Are Not a Separate Family

Service providers are a **role-position value** within B2B, not a separate product category.
Do not describe TexQtic as having a separate "service-provider platform." Service providers
participate in B2B using the service-specialist segments and the `Service Provider` role-position.

## 6. What Marketing May Do

| Allowed action | Guidance |
|---|---|
| Describe B2B and B2C as the platform's two commercial families | Accurate and complete as a market description |
| Describe White Label as a branded experience capability | "TexQtic's White Label capability enables operators to run branded storefronts and trade experiences" — correct framing |
| Describe Aggregator as a curated B2B discovery mode | "TexQtic's Aggregator mode connects textile-industry buyers and suppliers through curated discovery and qualified handoff" — correct framing |
| Use segment vocabulary from the B2B taxonomy in copy about who the platform serves | "From yarn manufacturers to garment makers, fashion designers to testing labs" — correct |
| Describe B2B as the textile-industry exchange family for manufacturers, traders, and service providers | Accurate summary of B2B's position |
| Frame intake and sign-up forms as interest capture | "Tell us about your business" or "Which segment describes your work?" is correct framing for intake |
| Use role-position vocabulary (manufacturer, trader, service provider) in copy about B2B participants | Accurate and supported by the locked taxonomy |

## 7. What Marketing Must Not Do

### 7.1 Do Not Present White Label as a Peer Family

**Incorrect framing example:**
> "TexQtic serves four types of businesses: B2B manufacturers, B2C operators, White Label partners,
> and Aggregator networks."

**Why it is wrong:** White Label is a capability overlay on B2B or B2C. It is not a fourth
business type or a separate product family in parallel with B2B and B2C.

**Correct alternative:**
> "TexQtic serves manufacturers, traders, and service providers across the textile supply chain
> through its B2B exchange platform, and consumer-commerce operators through its B2C platform.
> Our White Label capability lets operators run branded platform experiences."

---

### 7.2 Do Not Present Aggregator as a Platform-Owned Network Family

**Incorrect framing example:**
> "TexQtic's Aggregator network connects thousands of textile businesses through our AI-powered
> matching engine."

**Why it is wrong:** Aggregator is a bounded discovery and handoff capability. TexQtic does not
operate it as a platform-owned intermediary or a separate network family. Claiming "our matching
engine" implies platform-held ownership of transactions and a separate product taxonomy that does
not exist.

**Correct alternative:**
> "TexQtic's B2B discovery workspace helps qualified textile businesses find and connect with
> counterparties — from initial discovery through to handoff for trade continuity."

---

### 7.3 Do Not Present Enterprise as a Separate Product Tier

**Incorrect framing example:**
> "TexQtic offers B2B, B2C, and an Enterprise tier for large manufacturers."

**Why it is wrong:** Enterprise is depth within B2B — not a third commercial product. Presenting
it as a separate tier implies a product structure that does not exist.

**Correct alternative:**
> "TexQtic's B2B platform scales from growing textile businesses to large manufacturers and
> multi-tier supply chain participants."

---

### 7.4 Do Not Present Package/Plan as Family Identity

**Incorrect framing example:**
> "Our Professional plan is our B2B offering. Our Enterprise plan is for large traders."

**Why it is wrong:** Plan labels describe entitlement and feature access — not which family or
mode a participant belongs to. A B2B participant can be on any plan. Collapsing plan into family
identity misrepresents the platform structure.

---

### 7.5 Do Not Imply that Intake Forms Create a Classification Assignment

**Incorrect framing example:**
> "Sign up and we'll classify your business to determine your segment and pricing."

**Why it is wrong:** Intake forms collect interest signals, not canonical taxonomy assignments.
The canonical classification happens later, during platform provisioning. Implying that a form
submission produces a binding classification claim is both inaccurate and misleading.

**Correct alternative:**
> "Tell us about your business and what you're looking for — we'll use that to guide you to the
> right platform experience."

---

### 7.6 Do Not Describe Aggregator as a Separate Marketplace Competing With B2B

**Incorrect framing example:**
> "Choose between our B2B exchange or our Aggregator marketplace."

**Why it is wrong:** Aggregator is part of the B2B ecosystem, not a separate alternative
marketplace. It serves a bounded purpose — discovery and handoff — within the broader B2B
commercial context.

## 8. Operational Examples — Correct vs. Incorrect Copy

| Scenario | Incorrect version | Correct version |
|---|---|---|
| Describing who TexQtic serves | "B2B, B2C, White Label, and Aggregator businesses" | "B2B exchange participants and B2C operators, with White Label and Aggregator capabilities available" |

## 9. Boundary and Ownership Rules

| Boundary | Rule |
|---|---|
| Marketing owns | Public description, interest capture, campaign language, copy positioning |
| Marketing does not own | Canonical taxonomy assignment, canonical family classification, canonical package assignment |
| Interest capture forms | May collect business-context signals; must not imply they produce binding classification |
| Segment vocabulary in copy | May be used descriptively; must not imply Marketing is assigning a canonical segment to the reader |
| White Label and Aggregator in copy | May be described as capabilities/modes; must not be presented as separate family categories |
| Package/plan names in copy | May describe what features are included; must not substitute for family or capability identity |
| Where Marketing stops | When a prospect submits interest, classification authority passes to CRM normalization and then to provisioning. Marketing does not follow the prospect into those stages. |

## 10. Practical Implementation Guidance

### Website copy

- In hero and navigation sections: name B2B and B2C as the platform families. WL and Aggregator
  may appear as capability descriptions or feature highlights, not as family names.
- In "who we serve" sections: use segment vocabulary from the canonical list (§5.3). You do not
  need to list all segments — use the ones relevant to the target audience — but do not invent
  segment names outside the canonical list.
- In pricing or plan pages: describe what each plan includes; do not attach plan names to family
  names as if they are equivalent.

### Campaign language

- B2B campaigns may describe the full textile-industry participant range: manufacturers, traders,
  service providers, production-stage operators, testing labs, certification agencies, etc.
- WL campaigns may describe branded experience, operator controls, and white-label deployment
  features — positioned as an add-on capability, not a standalone product.
- Aggregator campaigns (if any) should focus on the discovery, qualification, and connection
  experience — not on TexQtic as a transaction intermediary.

### Intake and sign-up forms

- Use language like "tell us about your business" or "describe your area of operation."
- If you collect segment vocabulary in a form, label the field as "your primary area of focus" or
  similar — not "your segment classification."
- Do not present form completion as producing a TexQtic account type or canonical classification.
- After form submission, the prospect enters CRM normalization; that step is invisible to and not
  owned by Marketing.

### Segment vocabulary in SEO and content

- Use canonical segment terms (Yarn, Weaving, Garment Manufacturing, etc.) in content targeting
  specific textile-industry communities — this is accurate and consistent with platform taxonomy.
- Do not create custom segment names or aliases for SEO purposes that diverge from the canonical
  vocabulary.

## 11. Final Handoff Checklist

Before publishing any material about TexQtic's platform structure, apply this checklist:

- [ ] **Family count:** Does the copy describe exactly two commercial families (B2B and B2C)?
      Is INTERNAL absent from public-facing copy?
- [ ] **White Label framing:** Is WL described as a capability or overlay — not a peer family?
- [ ] **Aggregator framing:** Is Aggregator described as a discovery/handoff mode — not a separate
      marketplace or platform-owned network?
- [ ] **Enterprise framing:** Is enterprise presented as B2B depth — not a separate product tier?
- [ ] **Package/plan language:** Are plan names (Free, Starter, Professional, Enterprise) used to
      describe features and access — not as substitutes for family identity?
- [ ] **Intake form language:** Are forms framed as interest capture — not as classification events?
- [ ] **Segment vocabulary:** Are segment names drawn from the canonical list (§5.3) where used?
- [ ] **Service provider framing:** Are service providers described as a role within B2B — not as
      a separate product family?
- [ ] **Aggregator ownership:** Does copy avoid implying that TexQtic is a platform-held
      intermediary owning transactions between parties?
- [ ] **Canonical truth preserved:** Does this copy align with the family model and taxonomy
      described in this paper?

If all boxes are checked, the content is aligned with platform taxonomy truth and may be published.
