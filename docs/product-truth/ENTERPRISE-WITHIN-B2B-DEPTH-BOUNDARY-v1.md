## 2. Controlling Classification

The controlling classification is:

- enterprise is a depth posture within B2B operating reality
- enterprise is not a separate board-level mode
- enterprise is not a fourth or fifth platform pillar
- enterprise is not a separate governed commercial access model

The only governed commercial access models remain:

1. B2B Exchange
2. B2C Tenant-Branded Commerce
3. Aggregator Directory Discovery + Intent Handoff

Enterprise therefore exists only as subordinate depth inside `B2B Exchange`.

## 3. What Enterprise Means Within B2B

Within TexQtic planning truth, enterprise means increased operating complexity inside the B2B
exchange family.

That complexity may include:

- longer or more complex RFQ and quotation chains
- deeper negotiation continuation
- more complex approvals or internal operator workflows
- richer partner, compliance, or trade-governance expectations
- more complex commercial continuity around catalog, orders, and downstream workflow
- stronger expectations around integrations, auditability, and operational rigor

These are depth characteristics within B2B. They are not evidence of a separate commercial access
model.

## 4. What Enterprise Does Not Mean

Enterprise does not mean:

- a separate tenant mode parallel to B2B, B2C, or Aggregator
- a separate launch family outside B2B
- a separate pillar next to white-label or control-plane
- a distinct board-level product identity
- a license to split business-facing work into “B2B” and “Enterprise” as if they were different
  commercial access models

Enterprise-specific bounded units are allowed later only when they are explicitly framed as B2B
depth or B2B child continuity units.

## 5. Current Sources Of Drift

Current repo/planning drift mainly appears when tactical artifacts use `enterprise` as shorthand
for one reviewed B2B runtime branch.

Examples of the drift class include:

- bounded unit names such as `ENTERPRISE-RFQ-TO-NEGOTIATION-BRIDGE-CONTINUITY`
- tactical phrases such as `enterprise RFQ path`, `enterprise runtime`, or `enterprise redesign`
- older planning language that juxtaposes enterprise and white-label as if they were co-equal mode
  families

These phrases are sometimes useful as local shorthand for a reviewed B2B branch, but they must not
be read as evidence that enterprise is its own platform mode.

## 6. Lawful Interpretation Rule

When a later artifact uses `enterprise`, the lawful interpretation must be one of the following:

1. a B2B child path under review
2. a B2B depth posture requiring more complex continuity than the minimal B2B core
3. a bounded B2B-specific implementation or design slice

It must not be interpreted as:

1. a separate commercial access model
2. a separate planning pillar
3. a separate launch scope category outside B2B

## 7. Inheritance Rules For Later Units

Later family units must inherit all of the following:

1. B2B is the parent family.
2. Enterprise depth remains subordinate to B2B.
3. Enterprise-specific wording must not silently create a separate mode.
4. If a later unit touches enterprise depth, it must say what B2B child continuity or B2B depth
   it is addressing.
5. White-label overlay work must not use enterprise terminology to create a parallel operating
   family.
6. Tenant back-office, control-plane, catalog, orders, payments, messaging, and identity work may
   support enterprise-grade depth, but that support does not change enterprise classification.

## 8. Deferred To Later Families

This boundary note deliberately does not solve:

- white-label overlay normalization
- tenant back-office family definition
- control-plane family replanning
- catalog/discovery cross-mode family definition
- orders / checkout / post-purchase family definition
- payments / escrow / settlement family definition
- identity / tenancy / permissions / workspace family definition

Those families may later describe enterprise-grade complexity, but they must inherit the boundary
recorded here.
